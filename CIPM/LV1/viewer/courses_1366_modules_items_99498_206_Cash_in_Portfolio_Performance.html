2.06 | Cash in Portfolio Performance

Module 2: Performance Evaluation: Rate-of-Return Measurement2.06 | Cash in Portfolio Performance

Learning Outcome

The candidate should be able to:

  • calculate and interpret a portfolio rate of return

In a typical portfolio, such as a brokerage account, there is usually a provision for holding money that is not otherwise invested in securities in cash and cash equivalents. Such investments may be called the liquidity reserve asset, or simply cash. Income from dividends or coupon interest and sales of securities typically increase the value of the liquidity reserve, whereas purchases of securities typically decrease the liquidity reserve. When money is deposited to or withdrawn from the account, the money flows into or out of the liquidity reserve.

The value of the liquidity reserve is an important component in calculating portfolio performance, and care should be taken in its accounting. (Note that the Global Investment Performance Standards [GIPS®] require returns from cash and cash equivalents held in portfolios to be included in all return calculations [CFA Institute 2020].)
