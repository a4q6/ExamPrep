Module 2: Introduction to Benchmarks2.01 | Introduction

The Oxford English Dictionary defines a benchmark as “a standard or point of reference against which things may be compared.” In an investment context, a benchmark is a standard or point of reference for evaluating the performance of an investment portfolio. The selection of an appropriate benchmark depends on the objective and constraints that govern the portfolio’s construction.

An investment benchmark is typically a collection of securities that characterizes a manager’s preferred habitat of securities and risk. For example, an investor in German equities with characteristically small market capitalizations (and no other distinctive selection characteristics) might have a benchmark consisting of a broad portfolio of small-market-cap German equities. However, a benchmark can also be an absolute rate of return, or target return—for example, when the investment strategy targets achieving a specific rate of return and the strategy cannot be readily represented by a portfolio. Benchmarks can also take the form of peer group performance data against which an investment strategy is compared.

Benchmarks make it possible to determine how effectively active asset managers—managers who trade on perceived opportunities to earn superior risk-adjusted returns—have performed by measuring the effects of decisions to depart from benchmark weights. An ideal benchmark provides the manager and his investment clients with an objective means of evaluating how skillfully that manager implemented his investment strategy. Analysts can use it when attempting to identify active investment management skill. Well-chosen benchmarks allow returns to be accurately evaluated and decomposed. If the benchmark chosen is invalid, then all subsequent manager evaluation and analysis is incorrect. Throughout this learning module, we refer to the ideal benchmark as one that is “valid,” consistent with the literature in the field. Although a valid benchmark may be unattainable in certain circumstances, a performance analyst’s goal should be to use a benchmark that allows the most accurate evaluation of manager performance.

Benchmarks are extremely important to owners of capital. Benchmarks communicate information about a manager’s investment universe (the set of assets that may be considered for investment) and investment discipline. Managers often claim to beat “the market.” The benchmark defines the relevant market and allows analysis of the manager’s performance.

Benchmarks provide managers with a guidepost for acceptable levels of risk and return. They can be a powerful influence on investment decision making. As one investment authority has opined, “Benchmarks determine the performance of investment managers perhaps more than any other influence, including managers’ determination to succeed and the resources and skills they bring to this task. We in the industry have largely overlooked this fact, perhaps at our peril.”1

This learning module will help the reader understand benchmarks. Among the questions it will answer are the following:

  • How are benchmarks used?

  • What are the properties of a valid benchmark, and how can they be tested for quality?

  • What are the various types of benchmarks, and what are their advantages and disadvantages?

  • How are indexes constructed?

  • How are custom benchmarks constructed?

  • What are some alternative benchmarks?

  • What is the effect of poorly specified benchmarks on investment analysis?

The rest of the learning module covers the following: the distinction between benchmark and market index; an overview of benchmarks and their uses, types, desirable properties, and quality analysis; the use of peer groups as benchmarks; market indexes and style benchmarks; custom benchmarks; hedge fund and liability-based benchmarks; and problems with benchmark misspecification.
