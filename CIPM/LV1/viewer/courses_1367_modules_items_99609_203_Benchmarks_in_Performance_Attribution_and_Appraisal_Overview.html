2.03 | Benchmarks in Performance Attribution and Appraisal: Overview

Module 2: Introduction to Benchmarks2.03 | Benchmarks in Performance Attribution and Appraisal: Overview

Learning Outcome

The candidate should be able to:

  • describe how benchmarks are used in return attribution and performance appraisal;

This learning module gives a general overview of benchmarks, including their uses, their types, their desirable properties, and tests of benchmark quality.

An asset owner is the owner of capital, perhaps a pension fund, endowment or sovereign wealth fund. The type of benchmark chosen will differ depending on the asset owner’s characteristics. In a private wealth context, we would in general refer to the private investor as asset owner.

An asset manager is the professional manager of separate accounts or pooled assets structured in any of a variety of ways (e.g., a US mutual fund, a UK unit trust, a European Union Undertakings for Collective Investment in Transferable Securities [UCITS] fund, an ETF, or a hedge fund). Although the asset owner could manage assets in house, in general, we will think of the asset manager as an external investment manager to whom management of a part of the asset owner’s assets is delegated. The asset manager may have a mandate from the asset owner to follow either a passive or an active investment strategy.

  • Passive investment strategies prominently include indexing.3 Indexers seek to deliver returns comparable to those of a market index by investing in or replicating the index constituents.

  • Active investment strategies seek to use a manager’s insight to deliver returns in excess of those from a passive buy-and-hold strategy. These returns are referred to as the manager’s active returns, and the variability of the active returns is referred to as active risk.

Benchmarks are vital for performance measurement when the assets are actively managed. Benchmarks are also relevant when passive strategies are used, however, as we discuss next.

Benchmarks: Investment Uses

There are several uses of benchmarks in investment practice, including the following:

  • reference points for segments of the sponsor’s (investor’s or asset owner’s) portfolio;

  • communication of instructions to the manager;

  • communication of instructions to a board of directors (or any oversight group) and consultants;

  • identification and evaluation of current portfolio’s risk exposures;

  • interpretation of past performance and performance attribution;

  • manager selection and appraisal;

  • marketing of investment products; and

  • demonstration of compliance with regulations, laws, or standards.

Asset owner benchmarks need to be distinguished from asset manager benchmarks. An asset owner’s strategic asset allocation (policy portfolio) is the long-run allocation to asset classes consistent with the investor’s objectives and constraints. From this allocation, a benchmark, often in the form of an investable market index, will be specified for each asset class in the strategic asset allocation. That practice implicitly and reasonably specifies a passive investment in the asset class as a neutral comparison point in measuring the owner’s progress toward investment goals.

In general when we refer simply to benchmarks, the reference will be to benchmarks provided to managers that are appropriate references for their specific investment disciplines. Given the asset owner’s understanding of the manager’s investment discipline, the benchmark conveys to the manager the asset owner’s expectations about how the assets will be invested and their expected risk and return. By conveying these expectations, benchmarks provide accountability, so that if a manager’s security selection and subsequent performance frequently diverge far from the benchmark, it will be apparent that the manager’s investment approach is inconsistent with the stated investment discipline. Benchmarks also ensure a degree of fairness in the sense that the manager will not be held to standards that the asset owner’s board or consultants might arbitrarily impose.

Second, the benchmark communicates to the board and external consultants the manager’s area of expertise and how a manager should subsequently invest and be evaluated. In a multiple-manager fund, benchmarks convey the managers’ coverage areas, so that assets and securities that lack coverage or are overemphasized can be identified.

A third use of benchmarks is to identify and evaluate the manager’s risk exposures. Managers often describe themselves as “value managers” or “growth managers.” However, these terms are imprecise. An appropriate benchmark will have risk similar to the portfolio and be informative in revealing the manager’s active risk exposures, which should help explain the manager’s performance within his or her chosen investment style.

Another fundamental use of benchmarks is to attribute and appraise past performance and, in general, the consequences of the manager’s investment decisions. The benchmark helps the asset owner determine and evaluate the manager’s excess return (the difference between the portfolio return and the benchmark return, which may be either positive or negative). Return attribution identifies such things as whether the manager’s security selection, industry bets (in equity analysis), or yield curve positioning (in fixed-income analysis) have added value. What caused the manager’s performance to differ from the benchmark? If the benchmark is inappropriate, such differences may inaccurately reflect the manager’s decision process. In the context of return attribution the benchmark must reflect the manager’s decision process and ideally disaggregate into its constituent parts—that is to say, the sum of the parts should add up to the total benchmark return. Performance appraisal’s chief focus is to distinguish active investment skill from luck.

Accounting or adjusting for risk may be accomplished in a variety of ways. One appraisal tool known as the information ratio (IR) attempts to measure the value added per unit of active risk. To calculate IR, the mean portfolio return in excess of the benchmark return is divided by the active risk.4 If the benchmark is inappropriate, the calculated value of IR will not be informative. Thus, when benchmarks are used in performance appraisal, benchmark selection is obviously important.

Inappropriate benchmarks introduce noise into the investment manager assessment process and provide a misleading picture of manager performance. Good benchmarks enhance the effectiveness of manager assessment, whereas bad ones may lead to an inefficient or unintended allocation of assets and disguise managers’ contributions.5 As a result, benchmarks will also be instrumental in investment contracts that have an incentive compensation component that rewards outperformance (and potentially penalizes underperformance).

Furthermore, benchmarks play a role in the manager selection processes because they will influence the perception of a manager’s skill. The analysis of past performance will involve a qualitative assessment of whether the manager’s investment process is robust enough to produce repeatable outperformance in the future or, alternatively, whether a manager’s past underperformance is likely to persist.

Benchmarks are also used to market investment products to potential investors. The Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS®) require that if a benchmark exists, it must be included in a performance presentation with its description. If no benchmark is provided, a reason must be given. Marketing requires the communication and explanation of the investment process, of which the benchmark is an essential descriptor of the investment strategy and a crucial determinant of excess returns. Excess returns have been found to be a significant determinant of investor inflows, so the choice of the benchmark will influence the fund’s ability to attract new capital.

Lastly, benchmarks are used to demonstrate compliance with regulations, laws, and standards. Regulatory organizations use benchmarks as part of their oversight and surveillance, and as a result, benchmarks have become mandated in many jurisdictions. In 1998, the US SEC introduced a requirement that mutual funds self-designate a benchmark and present their historical returns alongside it in the prospectus. Many other jurisdictions now have similar rules. More generally, institutions are sometimes restricted from investing in specific instruments, such as below-investment-grade bonds. Benchmarks for such institutions have to be tailored accordingly.
