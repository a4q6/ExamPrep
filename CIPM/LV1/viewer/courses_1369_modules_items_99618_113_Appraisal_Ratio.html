Module 1: Investment Performance Appraisal1.13 | Appraisal Ratio

Learning Outcomes

The candidate should be able to:

  • calculate, interpret, and compare the Sharpe ratio, M2, the Treynor ratio, Jensen’s alpha, alpha, the information ratio, and the appraisal ratio
  • identify and justify appropriate uses of performance appraisal measures
  • calculate, interpret, and compare performance appraisal measures
  • describe how non-symmetrical return distributions affect appraisal measures;
  • analyze the determinants of the information ratio according to the fundamental law of active management

The appraisal ratio (AR) is a returns-based measure similar to the IR. It is the annualized alpha divided by the annualized residual risk. Both the alpha and the residual risk are computed from a factor regression. While the AR can be computed using any factor model appropriate for the portfolio, the measure was first introduced by Treynor and Black (1973) using Jensen’s alpha and the standard deviation of the portfolio’s residual or non-systematic risk. Treynor and Black argued that security selection ability implies that deviations from benchmark portfolio weights can be profitable and showed that the optimal deviations from the benchmark holdings for securities depend on what they called an “appraisal ratio.” The appraisal ratio is also referred to as the Treynor–Black ratio or the Treynor–Black appraisal ratio.

Recall that Jensen’s alpha is frequently determined by the coefficient estimate of the intercept in a market model regression as given previously as Equation 10 and repeated below.

rp(t)−rF(t)=αp+βp[rm(t)−rF(t)]+εp(t)

In this description, βp[rm(t) – rF(t)] reflects the contribution of the benchmark to the excess return of the portfolio. As before, Jensen’s alpha, αp, reflects the reward for active management in that it is the average return in excess of what is expected given the portfolio’s systematic risk or sensitivity to benchmark excess returns. The error term εp(t) is a random variable whose mean is zero, and the excess return that cannot be attributed to the benchmark for a given time period t is αp + εp(t). The larger the variance of εp(t), denoted by σεp2, the higher the unsystematic risk (sometimes called non-systematic or residual risk) of the portfolio and the higher the risk associated with active management.

Given the standard linear regression assumptions, σεp2 can also be estimated using Equation 17. The variance of the portfolio, σp2, is a measure of total risk that consists of two components: (1) systematic or market risk, and (2) non-systematic or company-specific risk. The systematic component is βp2σm2, and the non-systematic component is σεp2. Thus, non-systematic risk can be determined if the variance and beta of the portfolio and the variance of the market portfolio are known.

σp2=βp2σm2+σεp217

The coefficient of determination or R2 of a regression is a measure of how well data points fit a linear statistical model. In the case of the market model, it is the ratio of the explained variance or systematic risk to the total variance and can be determined using Equation 18. It follows that the proportion of unexplained variance or non-systematic risk of the portfolio’s return to the total variance is one minus R2, as shown in Equation 19. Thus, non-systematic risk can be determined if the variance of the portfolio and the R2 of the market model regression are known.

The appraisal ratio (AR) measures the reward of active management relative to the risk of active management, as noted in Equation 20. Based on sample estimates, Equation 20 can be expressed as Equation 21. Recall that σεp=σεp2 and σ^εp=σ^εp2.

AR=αpσεp20

For example, assume Portfolio J delivered 10.0% returns with volatility as measured by standard deviation of 16.8%. The market index returned 8.0% over the same time period with a standard deviation of 12.0%. Portfolio J has an estimated beta of 1.20 and the risk-free rate is 3.0%. Jensen’s alpha is 1.0%, αp = 10.0% – [3.0% + 1.20(8.0% – 3.0)] = 1.0% = 0.01. Non-systematic risk is 0.007488, σεp2 = 0.1682−1.202(0.122)=0.007488. The appraisal ratio is approximately 0.1156, AR^=0.010.007488=0.1156.

As another example, assume Portfolio K delivered 9.0% returns with volatility as measured by standard deviation of 12.0%. The market index returned 12.0% over the same time period with a standard deviation of 13.5%. A market model regression estimates beta of 0.80 for Portfolio K with an R2 of 0.81. The risk-free rate is 3.0%. Jensen’s alpha is –1.2%, αp = 9.0% – [3.0% + 0.80(12.0% – 3.0%)] = –1.2% = –0.012. Non-systematic risk is 0.002736, σεp2 = (1 – 0.81) 0.122 = 0.002736. The appraisal ratio is approximately –0.2294, AR^=−0.0120.002736 = –0.2294.

Exhibit 18 provides an illustration of the appraisal ratio. Assume three portfolios, D, E, and F, whose Jensen’s alpha‒residual risk combinations are shown in Exhibit 18. The appraisal ratio for each portfolio is the slope of the ray that emanates from the (0, 0) intersection of the axes through the point plotted for each portfolio. Based on Exhibit 18, Portfolio D has the highest appraisal ratio. The result is that, for a given level of non-systematic risk, Portfolio D has the highest return and for a given level of return, Portfolio D has the lowest non-systematic risk. Although Portfolio E has a positive Jensen’s alpha, it is lower than that of Portfolio D and its higher non-systematic risk results in an appraisal ratio lower than Portfolio D. Although Portfolio F has less non-systematic risk than both Portfolio D and Portfolio E, its negative Jensen’s alpha results in an appraisal ratio that is lower than both of these other two portfolios.

The appraisal ratio compares a portfolio’s Jensen’s alpha with its residual risk. Jensen’s alpha corresponds to the return that is not explained by the benchmark’s return. It results from choices made by the manager in an effort to outperform the benchmark. Residual risk reflects deviations in the difference between portfolio and benchmark returns. Thus, the appraisal ratio allows analysts to check whether the risk taken by a manager in deviating from the benchmark is sufficiently rewarded by the portfolio’s returns.

A portfolio’s appraisal ratio, unlike Jensen’s alpha, is invariant to both the amount of benchmark risk and the amount of leverage used in the portfolio.16 These are important advantages. It can be thought of as the Sharpe ratio of abnormal returns: Jensen’s alpha measures abnormal returns, whereas the appraisal ratio measures abnormal return per unit of active management risk. There is general agreement in the active portfolio management literature that the best measure of this benefit is the ratio of the portfolio’s alpha to the portfolio’s residual risk.

We have presented the appraisal ratio here as a function of a single factor model. When the benchmark portfolio does not adequately capture the portfolio’s systematic risk, a multifactor model might be more appropriate than a single-factor model.
